My Dissertation

In my book style dissertation, I propose a theory to better understand Latino political behavior. Latinos who remain in contact with others in Latin America have a specific set of attitudes that often aligns with the right in U.S. politics. Additionally, Latinos who have more transnational ties also spend more time online, which can lead to higher exposure to misinformation.

My Research Agenda

I am interested, broadly, in political psychology and political communication. Across different projects, I look at the political behavior of different groups.

Works in Progress

Rumors Across Borders

In my solo work, I explore the consequences of transnationalism on the political behavior of Latines in the U.S.

Angry White Parents

My co-authored work with Professor Izatt and Zoe C. Walker explores the effect of rhetoric about discrimination and privilege on the political mobilization of white people in the U.S..

Language and Misinformation

My co-authored work with Franshelly Martinez-Ortiz explores whether bilingual Latinos perceive messaging reliability differently based on the language of the message.

Democratic Threat and Mobilization

My co-authroed work with Professor Nicholas Valentino explores whether perceptions of threats against democracy in the U.S. mobilize Democrats and Republicans at similar levels.
